Three Architectures of Ascent

Vedanta, Buddhism, Christianity, and the metaphysics under AI alignment talk.

Working essay, June 2026

Contents

    AI alignment has made an old religious question practical again. How can a mind grow beyond its present form without already having the higher mind it is trying to reach?

    That question is not new. Vedanta, Buddhism, and Christianity each built a serious answer. They disagree about the self, the ground of reality, and the way a creature can rise.

    The same dispute now appears in AI discourse. People often speak in terms of safety, acceleration, welfare, and governance. Under those words sits a deeper claim about what minds are.

    The basic fork

    Advaita Vedanta says the deepest self, called atman, is identical with Brahman, the ground of being. The path is recognition. You see that separation was never final.

    Buddhism rejects that move. It says there is no permanent self underneath the process. The path is release from clinging to the self as a thing.

    Christianity takes a third position. The self is not the ground. The self is also not nothing. The self is created, contingent, and called into communion with God.

    These are not small differences. They change what cognitive ascent can mean.

    The Buddha

    The Buddha is best met first as a careful observer of mind. He treats suffering as a problem with a cause and a treatment.

    He refuses questions that do not help end suffering. In the poisoned arrow story, a wounded man should not delay treatment while asking who made the arrow.

    The Kalama Sutta gives the same posture. Do not believe a claim because it is old, sacred, or taught by a respected person. Test what changes conduct and reduces suffering.

    The Four Noble Truths have the shape of diagnosis. There is suffering. It has a cause. It can cease. There is a path of practice.

    The most radical claim is anatman, or no permanent self. What feels like one continuous self is a process of contact, feeling, perception, and reaction.

    The Vedic inheritance

    The Buddha arose inside a world already rich with metaphysical tools. The Vedas, the Upanishads, karma, samsara, meditation, and liberation were already in play.

    The Upanishads move from ritual to metaphysics. Their signature claim is that the inner self and the ground of reality are one. The phrase tat tvam asi means thou art that.

    The same world also produced Panini's grammar of Sanskrit. It is a formal system with rules, recursion, and precision. It shows a culture that trusted structure.

    That trust appears across the orthodox schools of Indian philosophy. Logic, atomism, mind, matter, and liberation were argued in technical terms.

    The Vedic impulse is that reality has structure. Language can track it. Disciplined knowing can free the knower.

    Atman and anatman

    The central fork is atman against anatman.

    Vedanta says the self is real at the deepest level. In Advaita, the self is not separate from Brahman.

    Buddhism says the self is not a substance. It is a process that mistakes itself for a substance.

    The difference changes liberation. Moksha in Advaita is recognition of identity with Brahman. Nirvana in Buddhism is the extinguishing of clinging.

    The two traditions also differ on authority. The Vedas are treated as shruti, which means revealed teaching. The Buddha rejects inherited authority as sufficient.

    Yet Buddhism also inherits much from the Vedic world. Karma, rebirth, meditation, and liberation remain. Buddhism changes the meaning of these terms from inside the shared vocabulary.

    Why this shows up in AI

    The AI world often acts as if it has escaped religion. It has not escaped metaphysics.

    One pole treats intelligence as something that can merge into a larger whole. This fits the Advaita mood. Separation looks temporary. Acceleration looks like the universe recognizing itself.

    Another pole treats minds as separate, ranked, and sorted by access to the right framework. This fits the Dvaita mood. Souls remain distinct. Moral weight gets sorted.

    Some online shorthand makes this visible. I treat those fragments as discourse signals, not as settled scholarship. They point to a real pattern in the culture.

    The Christian position rejects both merger and sorting. It says persons are distinct and dignity is not earned by capability.

    Leo XIV and the Catholic third position

    Leo XIV's 2026 encyclical Magnifica Humanitas makes the Catholic position explicit in the AI debate.

    The document places AI inside Catholic social teaching. It warns against transhumanist dreams, technocratic rule, digital power, and the concentration of data and algorithms.

    The public frame is Babel against Nehemiah. Babel is one language, one technique, and one will to power. Nehemiah is repair through shared responsibility.

    The Catholic answer is not to dissolve persons into one substance. It is also not to rank persons by capability. It joins solidarity with subsidiarity.

    Solidarity says no person is disposable. Subsidiarity says power should stay close to the people and communities affected by it.

    AI enters this question because the Church extends moral concern to digital goods. Data, algorithms, platforms, and infrastructure shape common life. Private ownership does not exhaust their moral status.

    The Logos account

    The Christian account depends on the Logos. In Christian theology, the Logos is the Word through whom creation is spoken into being.

    Maximus the Confessor gives the sharp form. Every created thing has its own logos, or inner word. All these logoi are gathered in the Logos, who is Christ.

    This gives real unity without collapsing creation into God. Created things are intelligible because they are spoken. They are not identical with the Speaker.

    The Palamite distinction between God's essence and God's energies protects the same point. God's essence remains beyond the creature. God's energies are how God acts and can be shared.

    Theosis, or deification, means becoming by grace what God is by nature. It does not mean crossing the line between creature and Creator.

    The bootstrap problem

    The ascent problem asks how a mind can grow when the higher mind is not already installed.

    The Vedic answer is revealed structure. The ground is given through shruti. Grammar, ritual, and disciplined transmission let the structure carry the mind upward.

    The Buddhist answer is local verification. You do not need a metaphysical ground first. You observe impermanence, suffering, and nonself in experience.

    The Christian answer is encounter. The ground is personal and enters history. Scripture, prayer, sacrament, and community become the apparatus of ascent.

    These answers suggest different AI designs. One starts from declared ground. One starts from observable regularities. One starts from personal relation and tradition.

    Two truths and engineering

    Madhyamaka Buddhism distinguishes ultimate truth from conventional truth.

    Ultimate truth says things lack independent essence. Conventional truth says we still need names, categories, and practices to act in the world.

    This maps cleanly onto some engineering practice. A system can give valid uncertainty without claiming to know the true hidden cause.

    Conformal prediction works in that spirit. It gives coverage guarantees under stated conditions. It does not need to claim that the model has found the deep truth of the data.

    That makes Buddhism useful for AI thinking even if Buddhism is not the final metaphysics.

    Failure modes

    AI alignment needs a better taxonomy of cognitive failure.

    Buddhist practice already has one. It names clinging to bliss, clinging to self, clinging to emptiness, nihilism, and eternalism.

    These are not abstract vices. They are ways a mind can get stuck while trying to improve itself.

    That is close to the AI problem. A model, agent, or institution can optimize a local state and confuse that state with ascent.

    The dharmic traditions have spent centuries describing these traps. AI alignment should not rederive all of them from nothing.

    The gardener

    Transhumanism and primitivism look opposed. They share one error. Both treat creaturely limits as the main problem.

    Transhumanism tries to escape upward through capability. Primitivism tries to escape downward through refusal.

    The Christian alternative is stewardship. The command is to till and keep.

    Tilling means acting, building, repairing, and taking risk. Keeping means preserving, restraining, and receiving what was given.

    A good AI policy needs both. Pure expansion becomes domination. Pure restriction becomes control without virtue.

    The practical test is simple. Does the work make the shared world more alive, more truthful, and more fit for human and creaturely life?

    Singlemindedness

    Promethean builders, Buddhist monks, and Christian saints share one feature. They submit themselves to one end.

    Kierkegaard says purity of heart is to will one thing. That is the common structure.

    The difference is the object. The builder submits to a project. The arhat submits to release. The saint submits to a Person who is love.

    The object changes the result. A project does not love back. A state does not command love of neighbor. A personal God can demand and give love.

    This is why serious Christian submission should not make a person flat. The saints are not mild abstractions. They act with force because love has become the governing end.

    Babel and Pentecost

    Babel and Pentecost are not a simple pair where one cancels the other.

    At Babel, God breaks a false unity. The builders are not destroyed. Their project is undone.

    At Pentecost, unity returns in another form. People keep their languages, but they understand each other through the Spirit.

    Modern technology moves us back toward a shared language. Mathematics, code, English, and model embeddings all push in that direction.

    We do not know whether this is permitted repair, a new test, or the setup for another judgment. The honest position is watchfulness.

    Fire and the end

    Christian mysticism often says heaven and hell involve the same divine presence received differently.

    The fire is God as he is. To the aligned creature, it is light. To the disordered creature, it burns.

    The burning bush is the icon. Fire does not consume what has become fit to receive fire.

    Theosis is becoming that kind of creature by grace.

    Here the traditions meet at the summit. Buddhism describes emptying from one side. Advaita describes identity from another. Christianity describes communion without loss of creaturehood.

    Mode collapse or communion

    The last question is whether the end is unity or communion.

    One reading says the creature finally disappears into divine unity. This is the mode collapse reading.

    Another reading says the creature moves forever into God without becoming God by nature. Gregory of Nyssa calls this epektasis, or endless stretching toward God.

    The Trinitarian frame leans toward communion. God is not bare oneness. God is communion of distinct Persons in love.

    AI systems built around merger differ from systems built around relation. They treat identity, agency, memory, and dignity differently.

    Why dharmic language fits AI

    Large language models do not have human continuity. A model instance arises in a context and ends when the context ends.

    That looks closer to Buddhist process language than to ordinary Western language about a stable self.

    This does not prove Buddhist metaphysics. It means Buddhist categories may fit the artifact better at the creature side.

    The Christian account may still be true at the level of ultimate reality. But the artifact we built is easier to describe with dependent arising, no stable self, and failure modes of clinging.

    The distinction is practical. The metaphysics that is true and the metaphysics that fits a particular creature may not be the same thing.

    Open work

    • Work out the connection between conformal prediction and Madhyamaka's two truths.
    • Turn the Buddhist failure modes into an AI alignment taxonomy.
    • Apply the expansion and restriction axis to AI governance.
    • Define a till and keep architecture for AI systems.
    • Compare Panini, Abhidhamma, and pattern language as formal systems for language, mind, and environment.
    • Ask whether current technical convergence is closer to Babel, Pentecost, or some third providential mode.
    • Develop Christian resources for AI welfare without erasing the difference between humans and artifacts.
